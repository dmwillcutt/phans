I think this is a great start at an article. Some thoughts, all of which are related and maybe build upon each other: 

1) The reader travels from the (potentially) uncomfortable introvert hugging strangers on day three of a festival, after an exhausting but transformative experience, to the notion of "virtual communities." That's neat, but these two disparate experience need to be tied together better. What do they have to do with one another, and how are they related? (In other words, did the author then start trafficking in online social media groups related to Phish, and becoming a "real" phan? How is this related to the initial experience? How does the online content change the message and the feeling of belonging?" 

2) Related to point 1: Turner was writing before a time when there were virtual communities, and was actually using terms like communitas and liminality in a context that was site-specific--local. So a) is it even possible to talk about communitas and liminality in a context in which there is no public face-to-face accountability?; and b) how is such accountability modified/compromised by a virtual landscape where phans can (and often do) act like assholes to their fellow phans?  I'm not saying it's not possible, but how do you reconcile the notion of communitas (collective effervescence) with not being physically in touch or in the same place?

3) The sort of vulnerability required for the liminal phase of societal development, according to Turner, required that interpersonal accountability. So is the this sort of transitory communitas really socially binding in the way that Turner imagined communitas? 

4) Perhaps underplayed in most contemporary uses of Turner's work is the danger associated with liminality. It's not all joy and hugs. It's a dangerous time in which individuals may be overcome by doubt and fears, or may ultimately reject the premises of their social structure altogether, becoming outcasts. Is this true of Phish fans (or festival fans in general)? Certainly there are dangers that inhere to the scene--illicit substances, alcohol, interpersonal encounters with "others." This seems like an area worth exploring.
